# **Section 4: What the Literature Agrees On, Where It Conflicts, and What's Missing**

## **4.1 Three Things the Literature Agrees On**

**Internal testing doesn't tell you whether a model will work in the real world.** This isn't a fringe view  it's the conclusion of four completely independent research traditions arriving at the same place. Christodoulou measured it (performance dropped from 0.72 to 0.54 when models were tested externally). Sullivan explained *why* it happens conceptually: researchers confuse "does well on held-out data" with "works reliably in practice," which are different things. Whalen identified the technical mechanism: when data preprocessing steps happen before the train/test split, information leaks across the split and inflates how good performance looks. Collins responded with a policy answer: external validation should be mandatory, not optional. When four traditions  empirical, theoretical, methodological, normative  reach the same conclusion independently, that conclusion is probably settled.

**The problem of dataset shift is well understood, but nobody agrees on how to fix it.** Multiple papers document the same core issue: a model trained on one patient population encounters a different population at deployment, and performance collapses. Wörheide showed this in clinical biomarker data. Springer and Doering showed it in immunological sequence data. Ben-David proved mathematically that it must happen under certain conditions. Subbaswamy and Saria argued the only lasting solution is to build models that understand *why* variables are related, not just *that* they are. The field agrees on what goes wrong. It doesn't agree on what to do about it. That gap between shared diagnosis and absent consensus on treatment is the most practically important thing to understand about this literature.

**Models learn the wrong patterns  and standard statistics can't fix this alone.** Emerson ran simulations showing that models learn spurious correlations  associations that exist in training data but don't hold at deployment because they were artefacts of the training context, not real signals. Springer and Doering's decoy experiment made this concrete: a model appeared to learn meaningful biological interaction patterns, but was actually memorising superficial sequence features that happened to correlate with the outcome in training. Whalen flags confounding as a systematic pitfall. All three agree that applying better statistical corrections doesn't solve this  you have to understand the underlying mechanism. The open question is how to do that without the prior knowledge that causal methods typically require.

## **4.2 Two Real Disagreements**

**Should we treat generalisation failure as a statistics problem or a causation problem?** This is the most important unresolved argument in the literature.

The statistical camp  Whalen, Collins, Christodoulou  says the problem is mostly about discipline and data quality. Get your train/test splits right, report results honestly, collect more diverse training data, and the problem is manageable.

The causal camp  Emerson, Arjovsky, Subbaswamy and Saria  says this misses something fundamental. Even if you handle all the statistical details perfectly, a model that learns associations will learn the *wrong* associations whenever those associations depend on context. The solution requires building models that understand causal structure, not just statistical patterns.

Both positions have empirical support. Neither has been tested head-to-head. The specific gap is easy to state: Christodoulou tests conventional methods and finds they fail, but never tries causal alternatives. Emerson tests causal modelling and shows it helps, but never compares it against a carefully implemented version of the statistical approach. So nobody actually knows, empirically, when better statistical practice is sufficient and when causal methods are genuinely necessary. That experiment has not been done.

**Can generalisation failure always be corrected, or is it sometimes just impossible?** Applied papers tend toward optimism: shift exists, but you can correct for it, and robustness can be improved with the right methods. Ben-David's theoretical work introduces a harder limit: when the training and deployment populations are sufficiently different, *no correction* strategy can recover good performance, because the distributions are simply too far apart for any adjustment to bridge the gap. The applied papers don't engage with this theoretical bound. The theory paper doesn't test whether the bound actually bites in real clinical settings. The consequence is that the field has no principled answer to a question that matters enormously for practice: when is it worth trying to adapt a model to a new context, and when is the honest answer that the model cannot work there regardless of what you do?

## **4.3 Three Things the Literature Has Missed**

**No one has built a framework for predicting failure *before* it happens.** The literature is sophisticated at documenting failure retrospectively  here is a model that didn't generalise, here is why, here is what the performance gap looked like. It's also reasonably good at proposing theoretical solutions. What's almost entirely absent is the prospective question: can you *estimate* the likely performance gap before deploying a model to a new context? Can you predict, in advance and for a specific target population, whether a model is likely to work? None of the fifteen papers surveyed provides a validated method for doing this. They can tell you the model failed. They cannot tell you, before you commit to deployment, that failure was predictable.

**Model monitoring after deployment is treated as an afterthought.** A clinical prediction model doesn't operate in a static environment. Patient populations shift. Treatment protocols change. Measurement technologies improve. Coding practices drift. A model that was well-calibrated at the time of deployment may be quietly degrading over months or years without anyone noticing until harm has already occurred. Collins mentions monitoring briefly. Wörheide flags it as desirable. But no paper in the corpus treats monitoring as a primary research question. What signals indicate that a model's operating conditions have changed? How do you distinguish real distributional shift from normal sampling variation? When should you trigger recalibration? What does responsible recalibration look like in a live clinical system? These are not implementation details  they are fundamental to whether deployed models remain safe over time. The literature's silence on them is conspicuous.

**Causal methods require a causal graph that usually doesn't exist.** The most constructive methodological contribution in the literature  Emerson's causal framework  points in a direction that appears genuinely right. But applying it requires specifying the causal relationships between variables before the method can help. In most real clinical ML settings, that prior knowledge doesn't exist in complete, validated form. Relationships between clinical variables are contested, domain-specific, partially understood, and require expertise that most ML teams don't have access to. The gap between "causal methods are the right approach in principle" and "here is how to actually apply them when you don't have a validated causal graph" is the most significant unresolved methodological problem in this entire literature.

## **4.4 The Core Research Gap**

The literature has established a lot. It has shown that standard models fail at deployment. It has characterised the mechanisms of dataset shift and proposed correction strategies. It has offered invariant and causal learning as more principled alternatives. It has mapped extreme boundary conditions where generalisation fails completely. It has defined minimum reporting standards. The field is diagnostically sophisticated  it knows, in considerable detail, what goes wrong and why.

What doesn't exist is a framework that does all four of the following things together:

1. **Estimates  before deployment  the likely performance gap** between the population the model was trained on and the population it will actually be applied to  
2. **Identifies which features are likely to remain predictive** across anticipated shifts, and which are unreliable because their relationship to the outcome is context-dependent  
3. **Gives practitioners a clear decision framework** when the gap is too large  whether to adapt the model, restrict its use, or not deploy it at all  
4. **Integrates with ongoing monitoring** that can detect distributional drift before performance has already degraded in practice

The theoretical components for such a framework exist, distributed across the literature. What doesn't exist is a version that is integrated, clinically validated, and operationally usable without requiring a fully specified causal graph or access to multiple independent training environments  resources that are almost never available in practice.

The gap is not about awareness. The field knows what the problem is. The gap is that no one has assembled the existing pieces into something that works end-to-end under realistic constraints. That precise characterisation matters: the contribution that's needed is not identifying a new problem, but solving a known assembly problem under conditions that actually exist in clinical practice.
