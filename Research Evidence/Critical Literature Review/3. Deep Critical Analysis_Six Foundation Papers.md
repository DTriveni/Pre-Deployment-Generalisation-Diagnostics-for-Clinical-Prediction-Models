# **Section 3: Deep Critical Analysis of Six Key Papers**

## **3.1 Christodoulou et al. (2024)  *Science***

This paper is difficult to dismiss. It's published in *Science*, uses data from five international randomised controlled trials, and asks a straightforward empirical question: do clinical prediction models actually work when tested outside their training environment?

The answer is stark. Performance dropped from 0.72 balanced accuracy during training, to 0.60 with cross-validation, to 0.54 on genuinely independent trial data  essentially the same as flipping a coin. This pattern held across four different definitions of treatment success and two different algorithm types. With 217 predictors and 1,513 patients, you can't explain this away as a small-sample fluke.

**But there are real problems with how far the conclusions are pushed.**

The biggest unacknowledged assumption is that schizophrenia is a reasonable test case for clinical ML broadly. It's diagnostically heterogeneous, and the drugs involved act on dopamine receptors  which is mechanistically quite distant from whatever is actually causing the illness. This may genuinely be one of the hardest clinical prediction problems that exists. Jumping from "models failed in schizophrenia trials" to "clinical ML models generally fail" is a bigger inferential leap than the paper earns.

There's also a critical ambiguity the paper never resolves: did the models fail because they *overfit* the training data, or because the statistical relationships they learned simply don't *transfer* to new clinical environments? These are different problems requiring different solutions. Overfitting calls for better regularisation and more data. Transferability failure calls for causal methods, domain adaptation, or invariant learning. Because the experimental design can't separate these two explanations, a reader genuinely doesn't know which problem they're dealing with  and therefore doesn't know what to do about it.

Most tellingly, the paper never tests anything from the distributional robustness toolkit. No importance weighting, no domain-adversarial training, no invariant risk minimisation. The conclusion that standard models are fragile is justified. The implicit suggestion that the problem can't be fixed is not  it's simply untested.

One additional note: "external" validation here means data from different pharmaceutical company trials, not from different hospitals or routine care. Real clinical heterogeneity is far larger. The failure documented may actually *underestimate* the real-world problem.

**What it contributes:** Establishes empirically that the problem is real and serious. It stops at diagnosis  it cannot say what a better approach would look like.

## **3.2 Whalen et al. (2022)  *Nature Reviews Genetics***

This is the most systematically organised account of *how* models fail technically. It comes with interactive computational notebooks  rare for a review paper, and genuinely useful. The five failure modes it identifies are demonstrated quantitatively, not just described: non-independent training examples, distributional differences, hidden confounders, data leakage in preprocessing, and class imbalance. The demonstrations are reproducible and striking  the dependent-examples pitfall can inflate a performance metric by more than 0.5, and leaky preprocessing can produce impressive-looking accuracy on datasets with no real biological signal whatsoever.

**The central limitation is that it operates entirely within a statistical framing.**

The paper's underlying logic is: fix IID violations (independence and identical distribution assumptions), and the model will generalise. That's useful but incomplete. A model can pass every check on Whalen's list and still fail at deployment because the deployment population simply differs from training in ways no preprocessing checklist captures. The Christodoulou failure, as far as can be determined, isn't caused by data leakage or class imbalance  it's caused by statistical relationships not transferring across clinical environments. Whalen's framework has nothing to say about that.

The treatment of confounding is the most specific weakness. Confounding appears as one of five equivalent pitfalls, but it's qualitatively different from the others. A model learning the relationship between age and outcome in a cohort skewed toward 40-year-olds has learned *that population's specific* age-outcome relationship. When deployed on a different age distribution, that relationship may not hold  not because of any preprocessing error, but because the relationship itself is age-dependent in ways the model can't account for. The paper's suggested remedies  including confounders as features, or using PCA to capture unmeasured structure  are reasonable steps but don't guarantee stability when the causal structure differs.

The core recommendation  make your train/test split mirror real-world deployment  is correct in principle and frustratingly underspecified in practice. Clinical deployment happens across many sites, over years, in changing contexts. The deployment scenario often isn't known in advance.

**What it contributes:** A rigorous pre-flight checklist for model development. The limitation is that passing the checklist doesn't guarantee the model will work once deployed. The gap between "passed the checklist" and "will work in the real world" is precisely where the research problem sits.

## **3.3 Wörheide et al. (2021)  *GigaScience***

This paper is included for a specific reason: one of its central findings directly contradicts a widely used practice in biomedical ML, and that finding is important enough that researchers in the field should know it.

The paper draws a distinction between two types of dataset shift that are frequently conflated. *Covariate shift* means the input features change between training and deployment, but the relationship between features and outcome stays the same. *Prior probability shift* means disease prevalence changes, but how the disease manifests stays the same. These require different corrections. Applying the wrong correction makes things worse.

The experiment tests this concretely: predicting smoking status from UK Biobank data, with an intentional gap in age distribution between training and test sets. Three approaches are compared  leaving the model unchanged, reweighting training data to match the test age profile (importance weighting), and removing age from features before training (deconfounding).

The striking result: removing age from features made performance worse in *every* configuration tested, including configurations with no distributional shift at all. Not marginally worse  consistently and meaningfully worse. This directly contradicts common practice.

**The main problem is the gap between what was tested and what's being recommended.**

The experiment involves one type of shift, one dataset, one direction (younger to older populations). Real clinical shifts are multivariate  age, ethnicity, comorbidity patterns, equipment generations, clinician behaviour, and regional disease patterns all changing simultaneously and only partially observable. The theoretical framework handles this in principle; the experimental evidence covers only the simplest possible case.

The importance weighting recommendation has a boundary condition that's acknowledged but not investigated: it only works if every subgroup in the target population appears at least somewhat in training data. The paper doesn't show how performance degrades as coverage drops  which is exactly the information a practitioner would need to decide whether to use the method.

The recommendation to use distributionally robust optimisation (DRO) as a forward-looking strategy is presented without any testing. Recommending an untested method goes beyond what the evidence supports.

**What it contributes:** Tools for correcting known, measurable shifts. It has nothing to say about shift that occurs after deployment in real time, when the nature and direction of the shift aren't known in advance. That operational question  detecting drift, characterising it, deciding what to do  is entirely unaddressed.

## **3.4 Emerson et al. (2023)  *Nature Machine Intelligence***

This is the most constructive paper in the corpus  the one that comes closest to saying "here is what to do instead."

The approach uses directed acyclic graphs (DAGs)  diagrams that map explicit cause-and-effect relationships between variables  to distinguish which relationships in a dataset are genuinely causal (stable across different environments) from which are spurious correlations (present in training data but likely to evaporate when context changes). Applied to immune receptor diagnostics, using T-cell and B-cell receptor sequence patterns to diagnose disease.

Three simulation experiments make the core point. When confounder distributions shift, balanced error rate nearly doubles. When training data has selection bias, a model that looks excellent during development performs at chance on unbiased data. And batch effects in immune receptor data are substantially harder to correct than in gene expression data  and attempts at correction risk stripping out real biological signal along with the noise.

**The simulations are both the paper's greatest strength and its most significant constraint.**

Because nobody knows the true causal rules governing real immune receptor data, the only rigorous way to test whether a causal framework helps is to build synthetic datasets where the ground truth is defined in advance. The problem is that these experiments test performance when assumptions are violated in *specifically programmed* ways  not in the chaotic, multivariate, partially-unobservable ways that real clinical data generates. The results show how the method performs against its own idealised scenarios, not against reality.

There's also an underexplored finding that deserves more attention than it receives. The paper distinguishes between *causal* prediction  inferring effects from causes, like predicting immune response after vaccination  and *anticausal prediction*  inferring causes from effects, like using receptor patterns to diagnose an existing disease. Anticausal models are shown to be less stable when input distributions shift, because they're working backwards against the causal direction of the data-generating process. Most clinical diagnostic tasks are anticausal. If anticausal prediction is systematically less stable, that's not a niche finding about immune receptors  it's a structural vulnerability of clinical diagnostic ML broadly. The paper identifies this and doesn't follow it to its logical conclusion.

The deepest problem is what might be called the causal graph specification issue. The whole framework requires mapping causal relationships between variables before it can help. In high-dimensional biological systems, the empirical basis for confidently ruling out specific relationships is often weak. Every missing edge in the graph is an assumption that two variables aren't causally related  an assumption that's frequently unjustified. All simulations in the paper use correctly-specified graphs, so the critical question  what happens when the graph is partially wrong?  cannot be answered from this paper.

**What it contributes:** The closest thing to a constructive solution in the corpus. The open problem it points toward  developing methods for learning or approximating causal structure from data when prior knowledge is incomplete  is a specific and tractable research direction.

## **3.5 Springer and Doering (2021)  *Briefings in Bioinformatics***

This paper is included for one reason: it's the only paper in the corpus that seriously confronts what happens at the *boundary* of generalisation  not new patients with familiar characteristics, but observations that belong to categories that simply didn't exist in training data.

The paper studies TCR-epitope binding prediction: whether a T-cell receptor will bind to a particular molecular target. The method (ImRex) represents amino acid sequences as a two-dimensional matrix of physicochemical properties and applies a convolutional neural network.

The key methodological contribution is an evaluation distinction most prior work ignored. *Seen-epitope* evaluation tests the model on epitopes it encountered during training, just with different receptor partners. *Unseen-epitope* evaluation tests on epitopes that never appeared in training data at all. On the first metric, models look good. On the second, performance drops dramatically  often to chance.

The decoy experiment makes this concrete and damning: several published models that appeared to perform well at binding prediction performed *equally well* when the epitopes were replaced with random nonsense sequences. Those models weren't learning interaction patterns at all  they were memorising receptor sequence features alone.

**The architectural and methodological choices are less well-justified than the evaluation findings.**

CNNs work well on images because of two properties: nearby pixels are related to each other, and the same feature at different positions is conceptually equivalent. Neither property obviously holds for an amino acid interaction matrix. Whether a hydrophobicity mismatch at position 3 of a sequence has the same biological meaning as one at position 7 is not at all clear. The method outperforms baseline, but without theoretical grounding, it's uncertain whether this result would hold for different sequence families or is specific to the epitope distribution in this particular database.

The negative training data problem is more serious than the paper acknowledges. Confirmed non-binding pairs don't exist in databases  only confirmed binding pairs do. So non-binding examples are generated artificially by pairing known receptors with randomly selected epitopes they're not *known* to bind. But TCR cross-reactivity is well-established  some of these "negative" examples are probably real binding pairs, mislabelled. The paper doesn't estimate how much contamination this introduces or what effect it has on the model's learned decision boundary.

The headline claim  that the method can extrapolate to unseen epitopes  comes with a substantial condition: it only works for epitopes within approximately one amino acid substitution of something already in training data. That's interpolation at the very edge of the training distribution, not genuine generalisation to novel categories.

**What it contributes:** Maps the theoretical limit of the problem. When target observations genuinely belong to new categories, standard approaches fail completely and even the best alternatives only work at the boundary of what was seen before.

## **3.6 Collins et al. (2024)  TRIPOD+AI, *BMJ***

This is the normative boundary within which all other research in this space must operate. It's not the most technically sophisticated paper in the corpus, but it carries the highest authority: published in *BMJ*, registered with EQUATOR, developed through a Delphi consensus process involving 200 international participants from research, clinical practice, regulation, and patient advocacy.

TRIPOD+AI is a 27-item checklist for reporting clinical prediction model development and evaluation  whether the model is traditional regression or modern ML. Compared to its 2015 predecessor, it embeds fairness considerations across 12 items, explicitly mandates open science practices including pre-registration and code sharing, and requires patient and public involvement.

**The core limitation is that TRIPOD+AI ensures transparency, not quality.**

A model can comply with every item on this checklist and still be deeply unreliable in deployment. The five schizophrenia models in Christodoulou et al. could have been reported in perfect TRIPOD+AI compliance and still averaged 0.54 balanced accuracy on independent data. The checklist makes problems visible. It doesn't fix the underlying problems.

The Delphi process, despite its strengths, has a structural bias toward conservatism. Items that are technically optimal but unfamiliar or complex tend to fail the 70% consensus threshold not because they're wrong but because consensus is harder to achieve for ideas that challenge existing practice. The fairness items that made it through focus primarily on reporting subgroup performance  necessary, but not sufficient to address why minority groups are structurally underrepresented in training data in the first place.

The most directly useful observation for research purposes concerns Item 26, which requires authors to discuss generalisation limitations. The problem is there's no defined standard for what adequate fulfilment looks like. A single sentence saying "this model may not generalise to other populations" satisfies Item 26 in exactly the same way as a detailed analysis of measured distribution shift, anticipated deployment contexts, and evidence-based transportability estimates. That ambiguity means the item cannot be meaningfully enforced or evaluated.

**What it contributes:** Defines the reporting floor any new research on prediction model robustness must meet. The constructive opportunity it creates is specific: defining what an *adequate* characterisation of generalisation limitations should actually look like  with measurable criteria, not just a requirement to acknowledge the problem exists.

## **Observations Across All Six Papers**

The two papers doing the heaviest lifting for the research gap are Christodoulou  which establishes the problem empirically  and Emerson  which comes closest to a constructive solution but leaves the causal graph specification problem unresolved. The gap between them, between *knowing models fail* and *having a practical framework for predicting and preventing that failure*, is the space the research is trying to occupy.

The overfitting versus transportability distinction raised in the Christodoulou analysis resurfaces implicitly across multiple papers and is never resolved. That ambiguity is probably the single most important unresolved diagnostic question in the whole literature  because what you think the problem is determines what you try to do about it.

The anticausal prediction finding in Emerson et al. feels consistently underweighted. If most clinical diagnostic tasks are anticausal  inferring causes from observed effects  and anticausal models are systematically less stable under distributional shift, that's not a niche finding about immune receptor biology. It's a structural problem for clinical ML as a whole, and the field hasn't seriously grappled with it.
